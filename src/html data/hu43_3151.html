    Junketsu no Maria (Maria the Virgin Witch / Sorcière de gré, pucelle de force)



    Maria the Virgin Witch (Japanese: 純潔のマリア Hepburn: Junketsu no Maria, subtitled Sorcière de gré, pucelle de force) is a Japanese manga series written and illustrated by Masayuki Ishikawa. Kodansha Comics USA licensed the manga and will released it in 2015. It will be adapted into an anime for the Winter 2015 season starting in January.

    Set in France during the Hundred Years' War, it follows Maria, who is one of the most powerful witches of her era. She intervenes against the warring nations by using her succubus and incubus familiars to manipulate the opposing factions, as well as large-scale illusions, all for the sake of helping the people and maintaining peace. As a result, she has gained the appreciation of several villagers and the hostility of the Church, which considers her a heretic. Yet Maria is still a virgin and her own familiars tease her about it. As news of her actions spreads, Archangel Michael focuses on Maria and rejects her interference in human affairs. After a direct confrontation, Michael ultimately decides that Maria will lose her magical powers if she loses her virginity and also forbids her from publicly using magic, sending an angel called Ezekiel to oversee this decree.

    I was raised in the Christian church and was even an ordained minister when I lived in Texas. I could quote Gospel straight out of my head. I am now a Pagan and a witch. I used to practice the Old Religion (Italian witchcraft from the 19th century) but have now expanded to modern witchcraft (known as Wicca, and formed in the 1960's)... because sometimes the old ways are just too dated. My gospel is now Aradia, the gospel of witches.

    So yeah, I can totally relate to everything in here. On the church side, they are totally over-the-top and melodramatic about it, though there is basis in the truth. On the witchcraft side there is even less realism, but the themes are cool. The persecution is real - that struggle is true. People seek the help of witches, then shun them immediately. Even today you can see how people will ask witches to divine their secrets, then will carry on with fear mongering of the same people they sought advice from.

    In case you're wondering, witchcraft is a nature-based religion which believes that nature is the Great Teacher. We learn the workings of the heavens by studying the workings of nature which was made by the same creative force. Pagan folk religion is basically what is now labeled as "witchcraft". If you've ever noticed the distinct differences between the Catholic church and the general "Christian" denominations, a great deal of that comes from the merging of Pagan folk religion into the early Catholic church in order to overtake it and create one state-sanctioned religion. It was all about politics, not religion. Have you ever wondered why Catholics worship the Saints, as various aspects of godhood? This is a Pagan idea. Why do Catholics celebrate Christmas in the middle of winter when all historians agree that Christ was not born then? In order to coincide with the Pagan yule season. Why is the Catholic sabbath on Sunday, when the Jewish sabbath observed in the Bible was always on Saturday? Because of the Roman tradition of Mithraic sun-worship. Why is the number 13 unlucky? Because that number has a long history in Pagan folklore as a lucky number. I could go on.

    That would be "Black Friday", which we now refer to as "Friday the 13th". Friday, October 13th of the year 1307 is when all the Templars in France were rounded up and imprisoned (then charged with "satanism"). That date was probably not coincidental, as the superstition of the number 13 predates it, and remember who chose that date to perform the roundup. But this is the direct explanation behind our "Friday the 13th" superstition.
